Methodology · 2025 ED.

HOW WE MEASURE

A working paper, not a press release. We publish our method before we publish our results.

Pluviometer is built on a deliberately simple premise: cities change slowly, and the changes that matter most happen below the threshold of news. We measure what disappears one building at a time, in the ordinary course of code compliance and capital flows, because that is where most of the loss actually occurs.

Our field stations operate on a continuous low-rise survey, augmented quarterly by satellite reconciliation. We do not use computer-vision inference for the record counts — every demolition is verified against a 2015 baseline raster, a 2020 raster, and a 2025 raster, by a human reviewer. This costs us speed; it buys us the right to publish a number that is harder to dispute.

We make no claims of completeness. The 47% figure is for the 14 surveyed districts, not the region. The regional picture is almost certainly less alarming in some metros and more alarming in others. We will publish city-by-city results before the end of 2026.

Our funding is partial grants, individual reader support, and one anonymous endowment — disclosed in full below. We accept no funding from state heritage bodies, developers, or firms in the construction supply chain.

01
BASELINE RASTER

Pre-1990 building footprint set, hand-corrected from historical cadastral records. Published openly.

02
CONTINUOUS FIELD SURVEY

Local researchers walk the surveyed district monthly. Each closure, demolition, or adaptive reuse is logged with photos and metadata.

03
QUARTERLY SATELLITE

Sub-meter satellite rasters are reconciled with field data. Discrepancies above 5% are re-walked.

04
PEER REVIEW

Each report passes through three reviewers: a regional urbanist, a methodologist, and a local practitioner.

05
OPEN PUBLICATION

Reports, data, and appendices are released under CC-BY-SA. No embargo, no redaction, no paywall.

About · The Editorial Project

A JOURNAL, NOT
A CAMPAIGN

Pluviometer publishes observations. We do not petition, litigate, or petition governments directly. We believe accurate measurement is itself an act of urban care.

WHY A RAIN GAUGE
FOR CITIES?

A rain gauge does not change the weather. It records the slow, accumulated, often-invisible shifts that, over time, become a flood. Cities change the same way: one building at a time, in the ordinary course of administration, rarely dramatic, almost never news.

Pluviometer is named for the device that measures what is not yet a crisis. We were founded in 2019 by a small group of urbanists, photographers, and data practitioners in Surabaya, working from the assumption that the most important work in any city is the work that prevents quiet loss from becoming total loss.

We are deliberately not a campaign, an NGO, or a think tank. We are a journal — an editorial project with a continuous survey practice. We publish. We wait. We re-measure. The discipline is the work.

Funding disclosure (FY 2025): 62% reader-supported subscriptions · 21% partial research grants (Packard Urban Futures, Jakarta) · 12% anonymous endowment · 5% honoraria and speaking. No state, no developer, no construction-supply-chain funding.
